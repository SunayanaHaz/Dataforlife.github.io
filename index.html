Where Technology Fails

An Accessibility Analysis of Real-World Websites (AccessGuru Dataset)

Team: Data4Lyf Track: DubsTech Datathon 2026 — Technology

Team Members: Sunayana Hazarika, JP Lai, Gaukhar Zharkeyeva, Jacob Edelson

Problem Statement

Technology is intended to be inclusive, yet many websites unintentionally exclude people with disabilities. These barriers are often invisible to designers and developers but create significant challenges for users who rely on screen readers, keyboard navigation, or who have low vision.

Using the AccessGuru dataset, we examined:

  • Where accessibility failures occur most frequently
  • Which types of violations dominate
  • Which pages create the greatest barriers for users

Solution Statement

This analysis prioritizes quality over complexity, combining simple analytical techniques with clear insights, actionable outcomes, and a focus on real human impact. Rather than building complex models, we emphasize interpretability, severity-based prioritization, and practical recommendations.

Dataset Overview

  • 3,524 accessibility violation records
  • ~590 unique web pages
  • 6 domains: News & Media, Government, Health, Education, Technology, E-commerce
  • Violations align with WCAG 2.1 standards and include frequency and severity indicators

Data Preparation

  • Standardized domain labels
  • Removed unsuccessfully scraped pages
  • Aggregated violations at page and domain levels
  • Verified consistency between severity score and impact label

Assumption: The dataset reflects observed violations only; findings represent patterns in observed accessibility failures.

Key Findings

1) Which Domains Have the Highest Accessibility Violations?

Accessibility failures are unevenly distributed. News & Media exhibits the highest concentration of violations, followed by Government and Technology.

  • Content-heavy sites introduce more accessibility risk
  • Publishing speed and volume may reduce accessibility checks

Implication: Accessibility risk increases with content volume and publishing speed, making governance especially critical for these domains.

Figure 1. Where Technology Fails Most — Total accessibility violations by domain.

2) What Violation Types Are Most Common?

The most common violations across domains include:

  • Color contrast issues
  • Missing or incorrect landmarks (region)
  • Missing link names
  • Duplicate HTML IDs
  • Heading structure problems

Implication: These recurring violations suggest workflow and design-system gaps rather than technical complexity.

Figure 2. The Most Common Barriers to Digital Access — Top accessibility violations.

3) Are There Patterns by Violation Category?

Observed patterns:

  • Syntactic violations dominate across all domains
  • Semantic violations are rarer but concentrated in Education and Technology
  • Layout issues appear consistently but at lower volume

Interpretation: Most barriers arise from incorrect HTML structure and markup. While fewer in number, semantic violations disproportionately affect screen reader users.

Figure 3. Heatmap — Domain × Violation Category.

Severity Matters More Than Volume: Risk Score

Not all violations are equal. Some pages contain fewer issues overall but include more critical violations, creating significantly greater barriers for users. To capture this, we introduced a severity-weighted Risk Score.

Risk Score Methodology

  • 5 × Critical
  • 4 × Serious
  • 3 × Moderate
  • 2 × Minor

Why this matters: Unlike raw counts, the Risk Score reflects user harm and helps prioritize remediation where it will have the greatest accessibility impact.

Figure 4. Severity-Weighted Risk Score Ranking — pages ranked by estimated user harm.

Risk Score Insights

  • A small number of pages account for a large share of critical issues
  • High-risk pages often combine poor contrast, missing landmarks, and broken structure

Hidden Patterns: Clustering

To identify recurring accessibility failure patterns beyond individual violations, we clustered pages based on the types and frequency of violations they contained.

  • Method: KMeans clustering (k = 5) on page-level violation profiles
  • Scaling: StandardScaler to prevent large counts from dominating similarity
  • Quality metric: Silhouette score = 0.334 (moderate separation)

Five clusters emerged. Four represent distinct, recurring “problem families,” while one cluster contains low-violation or mixed-pattern outliers.

Problem Families

  • Contrast-Heavy Pages: dominated by color contrast violations (harmful for low-vision users)
  • Navigation Confusion Pages: missing landmarks/regions (screen reader users get lost)
  • Structure-Broken Pages: duplicate IDs and broken HTML (assistive tech misinterprets structure)
  • Heading Disorder Pages: incorrect heading order (users cannot skim or understand structure)

Implication: These clusters enable targeted, repeatable fixes rather than addressing violations one-by-one.

Human Impact

Accessibility failures disproportionately affect real people:

Violation Type Who It Harms Why
Color contrast Low-vision users Text becomes unreadable
Missing link names Screen reader users Links lack meaning / context
Missing landmarks Blind users Page navigation breaks
Duplicate IDs Assistive technology users Page structure collapses / becomes ambiguous

These issues create invisible but significant barriers to access.

1) Fix color contrast

Enforce WCAG contrast thresholds with automated checks in design tools and CI pipelines to prevent regressions before deployment.

2) Add semantic landmarks

Implement consistent semantic landmarks (<nav>, <main>, <header>, <footer>) across templates to enable reliable navigation for screen reader users.

3) Ensure meaningful link text

Replace vague or generic link text with descriptive, action-oriented labels and ensure linked images include alt text to provide clear context for screen reader users.

4) Validate HTML structure

Enforce HTML validation standards to ensure unique element IDs and a logical heading hierarchy, improving interpretation by assistive technologies.

Best-case impact: Addressing these four practices could prevent nearly 80% of observed violations (2,802 of 3,524), assuming consistent implementation across templates and components.

Conclusion

Accessibility violations are not rare or random. They follow clear patterns, affect specific groups, and can be measured, prioritized, and fixed. By combining data-driven analysis, severity-based ranking, and human-centered interpretation, this project shows exactly where to begin.

Accessibility is not a niche compliance issue—it is a measurable design responsibility.

Limitations

This analysis reflects observed violations from successfully scraped pages. Counts may be influenced by page volume, crawl depth, and site structure. Findings should be interpreted as patterns and relative risk indicators, not exhaustive audits of individual organizations.
